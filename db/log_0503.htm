For the record, we may be crazy about words, but those are not the kinds of neologisms we collect here. We use neologism to mean a new word, regardless of how many people (if any!) use it. That said, we don't include every word submitted, based on the rules outlined in our submission FAQ.

Goodbye, Dolly - 3/23/05 - 8:21 pm
Yesterday's post reminded me of a poem I once wrote about Dolly Pentreath , the last native speaker of the original Cornish language .
The Last Cornish Woman's Lament

Thomas had been hallowed these ten years gone
And she missed his soft voice murmuring at dawn
As much as she missed his lovemaking.

Worse than the falling darkness that afflicted her sight
Worse than the fading hearing that confused the sounds of night
Was the silence her throat swallowed and her own vocal aching.

She tried to speak again the word for the fading of the sun
Its sound had held a mystery when she was young
Now she was left with sunset and it did not suit her.

Lost to her lips were Thomas and her tongue
Lost were the words of the lullabies her mother had sung
Gone was the language of love with which Thomas had wooed her.

English had invaded her mind like it had invaded Kerno
She had no words left to express her sorrow.

Language Death - 3/22/05 - 7:45 pm
Terry Leonard of the Associated Press has written a compelling article, Knowledge Fades As African Languages Die:
A U.N. Conference on Trade and Development report on protecting traditional knowledge argues that beyond a devastating impact on culture, the death of a language wipes out centuries of know-how in preserving ecosystems - leading to grave consequences for biodiversity.

The United Nations estimates half of the world's 6,000 languages will disappear in less than a century. Roughly a third of those are spoken in Africa and about 200 already have less than 500 speakers. Experts estimate half the world's people now use one of just eight languages: Chinese, English, Hindi, Spanish, Russian, Arabic, Portuguese and French.

Malcontent Management System - 3/18/05 - 5:32 pm
The great part about writing my own content management system to run this site is that I can do all sorts of neat things that I couldn't do with an off-the-shelf system, such as having dynamically updated pages with unique formats (e.g., Conlang Survey Stats, Conlangs by Natural Language Influences, Books by Year Published). The bad side of this is that -- unlike at work -- I have no help with quality assurance, and if I don't find and fix a bug then it remains unfixed.

Well, for a long time we've been plagued by mysterious truncations of submissions. It finally dawned on me yesterday out of the blue that the problem was HTML tags with equals signs (e.g., <A HREF="foo.htm">), since the equals sign is a delimiter that separates field labels from field values in people's submission forms. I double-checked the code, and sure enough, that was the problem. Then I looked at the date of the original procedure: I had written it 3/17/02. So we lived with that bug for three years to the day. Argh.

Twenty-Six Counties - 3/17/05 - 9:54 pm
I was curious about which languages used all 26 letters of the modern Latin alphabet , and Wikipedia had most of the answer (I had to add Afrikaans to the article). At least 12 languages use all the letters: Afrikaans, Catalan, Danish, English, Finnish, French, German, Kurdish, Norwegian, Portuguese, Slovak and Spanish. Interestingly, only Afrikaans and English use just the 26 letters with no extended characters (Afrikaans because it uses 'y' instead of Dutch 'ij').

Why was I curious about this subject? IÂve often wondered if an auxlang should use all 26 letters or should use fewer, since obviously it should have a simple phonology. Right now, each of my fauxlangs settles for fewer than 26. I do have one language, a sketch, that uses all the basic letters: Pidanjinu, which cheats by having three clusters -- 'cy' /ky/ as in 'cute', 'qu' /kw/ as in 'quote', 'xs' /ks/ as in 'tux'. I am still contemplating what might be a good 26-letter design for an auxlang.

Short Stack - 3/06/05 - 1:55 pm
When I first translated the Langmaker motto into Fith, I made the typical human mistake of translating every word:
Shi shtring shoungh ke lo plaiÃ± lo ke sthem shi sren emn vo dyainmh meeÃ± o
you people vice the of concealing of the join you language a (it) makes when !
You join the people of the vice of concealing, when you make a language!
In Fithian discourse, it is considered poor style to reuse a recent word. Fith imperatives require the pronoun "you" (e.g., "You run!" not "Run!"), but it was poor form for me to repeat the pronoun, shi, for both sentences when it can easily be accessed using a stack conjunction. Additionally, it is good Fithian style to begin a paragraph with the last prominent noun (in this case, sren, "language"). Accordingly, I had to retranslate as follows:
Sren shi du shtring shoungh ke lo plaiÃ± lo ke sthem lonh shen emn vo dyainmh meeÃ± o
language you *dup people vice the of concealing of the join *counterrotate *swap a (it) makes when !
You join the people of the vice of concealing, when you make a language!
If any of you out there want to try and translate the Babel Text into Fith, I certainly encourage you to give it a try using the Fith-English online translator.

Fith Amendment - 3/05/05 - 1:13 pm
Last week I participated in the latest translation relay using Fith. I'm happy to report that Jan van Steenbergen says he was able to successfully translate from Fith. You'll have to wait until the end of the relay though to see my text.

In the process of working on the relay, I dramatically expanded the language and updated the Fith web pages. Highlights:
  • 10,000 word vocabulary
  • Online Fith-English translator
  • Expanded and revised stack operators
  • Expanded pronoun system & plurality and tense markers
  • No more hand signals.
The hand signals (allegedly to indicate part of speech) weren't in my first draft of Fith but were added in a second draft. Ray Brown in CONLANG-L commentary correctly indicated that they were a superfluity, and I removed them.

I added new "placeholder conjunctions". This type of operator searches the stack down for the first occurrence of the placeholder word nyun. Every subsequent element of the stack is then put in a series representing the relationship glossed (e.g., "and", "or", "nor"):
  • drumh (A nyun X Y Z -- A "X, Y and Z") marks a simple series of nouns
  • tuumnh (A nyun X Y Z -- A "either X, Y or Z") marks an "either...or" series of nouns
  • dwoumnh (A nyun X Y Z - A "neither X, Y nor Z") marks a "neither...nor" series of nouns.
As a result of this new construction, I revised dzhi to now copy the stack item above nyun to the top of the stack. (A B nyun X Y Z - A B nyun X Y Z X) Before, dzhi required a numeric argument, which seems less naturalistic than using a placeholder.

You can play with these and other Fithian operators using the new online translator. I tested it under FireFox and IE. It handles all the stack operators. I've written and tested 14 sentences using it. It's one thing to talk about stack-based conlangs -- quite another to get to play with one. I hope a few of you will experiment with the translator to play with Fith words. Best of all, they're stackable!

Reckless Wordsmithing - 3/03/05 - 1:03 pm

Americans have had a reputation for reckless wordsmithing ever since Thomas Jefferson was denounced in the London Review, in 1787, for the crime of coining belittle. "For shame, Mr. Jefferson!" thundered one 18th-century critic, as H.L. Mencken recounts in The American Language (1936 edition). "O spare, we beseech you, our mother-tongue!"
