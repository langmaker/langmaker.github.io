Before beginning, please review the pronunciation system of Kali-sise.  

The following lessons consist of three sections each:
The Root Words section introduces six root words for you to memorize.
Grammar introduces a key grammatical concept for you to learn and develop your own sentences demonstrating.
Compounds provide examples of words derived from all the roots covered up to and including this lesson.  You don't need to memorize these, but you should carefully review them and see if you can recognize the meaning from the roots.

Lesson 1

Root Words

kune [From Latin 'contra-', extant in English, et al.] Opposite extreme, polar opposite -- the direct contrast.
lete [From Romance -el, -et, -elet, extant in English -let ('booklet'), -ette, et al.] Little -- a small amount or duration {diminutive}.
nale [Latin 'mare', extant in Russian /mor/, Spanish /mar/, English 'maritime', German 'Meer', Italian 'mare', Esperanto 'maro' and Novial 'mare'.] Ocean, sea -- a very large body of salt water.
pe [From Latin 'persona', extant in Romance (Spanish, Italian, et al), Germanic (English, German, et al) and Russian, with initial /p-/ in Hindi /puruc/.] Person, individual -- a human being.
sili [From Hindi /shiryi/, with initial /shi-/ in Mandarin Chinese.] Bird -- warm-blooded egg-laying vertebrates of the class Aves characterized by feathers and forelimbs modified as wings.
tuna [From Romance augmentative -on-, extant in English 'canton', 'carton', 'fronton', 'panettone', 'spontoon', 'teston', et al.] Much -- a great amount or extent {augmentative}.

Grammar

If no verb is present, the verb "to be" is assumed. A verb takes any number of case phrases, each specifying a detail describing the action of the verb. Each case phrase can consist of a noun followed by a case particle. The most important case particle is the nominative particle, pi, which specifies the subject of the sentence.

Sili pi.
bird NOM
A bird exists.

Nale pi.
sea NOM
A sea exists.

Pe pi.
person NOM
A person exists.

Grammar

A vehicle is a tool.

Pitakesulina pi sinata pi.
alphabet NOM tool NOM
An alphabet is a tool.

Lesson 3

Root Words

Grammar

Any noun can precede another noun, in which case it treated as an adjective. In fact, in general in Kali-sise, modifiers precede the words that they modify; for instance, you can think of a noun as modifying the case particle it precedes.

Tuna sunu pi.
much sound NOM
There is a big sound.

Lete sili pi.
little bird NOM
There is a little bird.

When a number proceeds a noun, it should be interpreted as indicating the ordinal position of that noun (e.g., one > first, two > second, three > third, etc.).

Tu lepeti pi.
two reptile NOM
There is a second reptile.

Tu nasini pi nuni pi.
two vehicle NOM absence NOM.
The second vehicle is missing.

A person measured a reptile.

Nu lele pi sisi se ti nine ke.

One male moved the third fish.

Root Words

kapina [From Mandarin Chinese /kapyan/ with initial /k/ in Greek 'khartes', extant in Russian, Hindi, Germanic (English, German, Dutch), Romance (Latin, Spanish), auxiliaries (Esperanto, Novial), .] Card -- thin cardboard, usually rectangular.
lupete [From German 'Luft', extant in English borrowed term 'Luftwaffe'. Close in form to Dutch 'lucht'.] Air -- a mixture of gases (esp. oxygen) required for breathing, the region above the ground.
nasiku [From Russian /nasikoma/.] Insect -- small air-breathing arthropod of the class Insecta, with adult stage having three pairs of legs.
pete [From Indo-European *ped-, with initial /p-/ in Hindi /pamv/; *ped- survives in English 'pedestrian', Germanic *fot- (English foot) and Romance /p-d-/ (Spanish, Italian, Esperanto, Novial).] Foot -- the pedal extremity of the inferior limb in vertebrates.
sutale [From Sanskrit 'sutram', "thread", and Latin 'sutus', past participle of 'suere', "to sew" (extant in English 'suture'), all ultimately from Indo-European *syu-, "to bind, to sew". (Inspired by Acadon 'sutra'.)] Thread, filament -- something shaped like a long thin line, with its width almost insignificant compared to its length.
telule [From Latin 'terror', extant in Spanish, English, Italian, et al.] Fear -- an emotion experienced in anticipation of some specific pain or danger.

Grammar

When se is used to convert a noun into a verb, it creates an active-voiced verb. Typically English verbs need to be split into parts: "flies" = "moves through air", "swims" = "moves through water", etc.

Lepeti pi telule se pe ke.
reptile NOM fear VB person ACC
A reptile frightens a person.

Sili pi sisi se lupete li.
bird NOM movement VB air LOC
A bird moves through the air.

Nine pi sisi se pata li.
bird NOM movement VB water LOC
A fish moves through the water.

Grammar

The final of the six case markers is the ablative, which is marked by na and acts as a "catch-all" case: it can often be translated into English as "in a ____ manner" or "-ly". Unfortunately, because of its semantic flexibility, it is the most difficult case to translate back into idiomatic English.

Nasini pi sisi se tata na.
vehicle NOM movement VB amphibian ABL
The vehicle moves in an amphibious manner.

Pukuse pi pukuse se sasale ke sepete na.
fire NOM fire VB tree ACC blossom ABL
The fire blossomingly burned the tree. [figuratively:] Flames burst like flowers as they burnt the tree.

Lele ke lene se pepe na.
male ACC learning VB creation ABL
A male learns by creating.

The six case markers are derived from the Kali-sise word for alphabet, pitakesulina, which exhaustively illustrates all the sounds of the language. To review, pi is the nominative, ta the genitive, ke the accusative, su the dative, li the locative and na the ablative.

Le pi lene se kutu ta pe ke tata su sasale li pitakesulina na.
1ST NOM learn VB eight GEN people ACC amphibian DAT tree LOC alphabet ABL.
I taught eight people about amphibians in a tree proceeding alphabetically.
